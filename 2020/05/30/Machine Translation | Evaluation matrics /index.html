Machine Translation | Evaluation Matrics

>> Evaluation of machine translation

Subjective evaluation

The ALPAC report >> Pierce et al., 1966

  • Two major recommendations:

    • Fund research in (pure) linguistics;

    • Fund research in how to reduce translation time and how to reduce translation costs.

    ​ 由于当时机器翻译才刚起步，需要投入大量的财力人力来研究，这两项建议导致投资者中止了所有投入，严重阻碍了机器翻译在美国的发展（欧洲、亚洲似乎影响不大）

    有几篇关于这个report的负面评论：

    1. John Hutchins, ‘ALPAC: the (in)famous report’, MT News International 14:9-12, June 1996
    2. Christian Boitet, Factors for success (and failure) in Machine Translation – some lessons of the first 50 years: Fifth Machine Translation Summit, Luxemburg, 1995.
    3. Zbigniew Pankowicz, Commentary on ALPAC Report, “Language and Machines; Computers in Translation and Linguistics”, 1967.

  • Two criteria

    • Intelligibility / Fluency
      • How natural is a translation? How fluent? How close to what native speakers of the target language would say?
      • Scale: 1~9 and 9 is the most intelligible
    • Fidelity / Adequacy
      • How much of the information contained in a text is carried over through translation? Is there some piece of information missing in the translation? Was some information added by translation?
      • Scale: 0~9
      • 1~9 is from less infomative to extremely (over) informative. 0 is the best I think

Objective evaluation

mWER

multiple reference Word Error Rate >> (Niessen et al., 2000)

  • 第一次提出用distance来evaluate的是 (Thompson, 1991)

  • multiple reference WER 定义为:

    The minimal ratio of edit distance between the candidate translation and the reference over the length of the reference.

    1. Compute the Levenshtein edit distance (insertion, deletion, substitution all have a cost of 1) between the candidate sentence and each reference
    2. Divide by the length of the reference
    3. Take the minimum over all values for all references

    e.g.  Candidate: I would like a room with a view on the sea.

       Reference 1: Can I have a room with a view?
       Reference 2: Do you have a room with a sea view?

       Dist (C,R1) = 7 / 9
       Dist (C,R2) = 8 / 10

       mWER = min (7 / 9, 8 / 10) = 7 / 9 = 0.777

TER

Translation Edit Rate >> (Snover et al., 2006)

  • TER is commonly used when MT output is produced for post-editing, as it can help to estimate the amount of work your translators will have to do to bring the MT up to human quality..

  • TER is defined as the minimum number of edits needed to change a hypothesis so that it exactly matches one of the references, normalized by the average length of the references. BLEU

BiLingual Evaluation Understudy >> (Papineni & al., 2002)

  • BLEU is defined as the product of a brevity penalty to penalise shorter candidate sentences and the geometric mean of all n-gram precisions up to a certain order (usually 4)

  • Brevity Penalty (BP)

  • Modified n-gram precision

  • BLEU score

  • E.g.  Candidate: The cat eats the little mouse in the little garden.

       Reference 1: The cat eats the small mouse in the little yard.
       Reference 2: The small mouse is eaten by the cat in the small yard.

       P1 = 9 / 11
       P2 = 5 / 10
       P2 = 4 / 9
       P2 = 2 / 8

       BLEU = 46.17 (over 100 points)

  • sacreBLEU

    A tool which allows researchers to download standard testbeds for machine translation and run BLEU on state-of-the-art results and their own results.
    In this way, scores can be compared in a reliable and reproducible >way.

NIST
  • NIST is a method for evaluating the quality of text which has been translated using machine translation. Its name comes from the US National Institute of Standards and Technology.
  • It is based on the BLEU metric, but with some alterations. Where BLEU simply calculates n-gram precision adding equal weight to each one, NIST also calculates how informative a particular n-gram is. That is to say when a correct n-gram is found, the rarer that n-gram is, the more weight it will be given.
RIBES

Rank-based Intuitive Bilingual Evaluation Score >> (Hideki et al., 2010) & available here

  • better takes into account the order of words to be sensitive to differences in syntax between languages
METEOR

  • http://www.cs.cmu.edu/~alavie/METEOR/

  • Tries to take synonymy into account; needs extra knowledge data
