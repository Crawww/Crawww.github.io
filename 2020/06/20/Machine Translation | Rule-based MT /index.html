Process & Technology

  • Technology at that time:

    • Assembly language: 使用汇编语言，无面向对象的程序语言
    • No database management system

Difficult points for first-generation MT to get a good translation

  • 一个好的翻译，往往需要根据习惯对词语进行词性转换，但在双语词典中，词性是相对应的 (verbs for verbs and nouns for nouns)
  • 有些词应该组合起来翻译，比如特定动词与特定对象相联系可翻译为一个词 (要求一定的语法知识)，但词典中一般找不到这样的翻译
  • 有时动词和对象之间的距离较远 (长距离依赖 Long distance dependency)，查找字典的方法无法识别 (翻译)

Representations for Second generation MT

Pivot Representations

  • Warren Weaver’s memorandum (Philosophico-linguistic ideas)

    There are four proposals, the fourth proposal was that there may also be linguistic universals underlying all human languages which could be exploited to make the problem of translation more straightforward

    • there exists a common ground to all languages
    • this common ground is logical
    • source text >> universal representation >> target text

  • Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646-1716) (Philosophico-linguistic ideas) (Root)

    • A German philosopher and mathematician (inventor of differential calculus) conceived a language that would assign symbols to each element in the universe, or each elementary concept in the human mind, an alphabet of the human thought.

  • Anna Wierzbicka (1938-) (Linguistic research)

    • Semantics: primes and universals, 1996
    • Tries to extract the semantic primitives that constitute the meaning of words.
    • She translated some short texts into semantic primitives (e.g. the Lord’s Prayer)

  • Attempts in the history of MT

    • at CETA, in France, to translate texts in chemistry from Russian into French. The basic idea was to give a number to each Russian entity or predicate, and to map each French lexical unit onto these symbols.

  • Impracticality of a universal pivot language

    • Practical reason: too hard and too long for human beings to do
    • Theoretical reasons: languages do not map and segment the world in the same way neither in linguistic categories (gender, num-
      ber, tenses, illocutionary force, etc.) nor in concepts (vocabulary) (Mounin, Theoretical problems of translation (in French), 1976)

Linguistic Representations

Constituency representations (and syntactic functions)
  • Constituents (grammatically complete groups of words)
    • Noun Phrase (NP)
    • Verb Phrase (VP)
    • Prepositional Phrase (PP)
    • Sentence (S)
  • Tree representations and projective
  • Phrase structure grammar
  • Immediate constituent analysis
    • People: L. Bloomfield, Z. Harris, N. Chomesky
    • Book: N. Chomsky, Syntactic structures, Mouton, 1955
    • Paper: N. Chomsky, Bare Phrase Structure
  • Syntactic functions (Grammatical relation)
    • Grammatical interpretation of the relations of the phrases to the verb of a sentences
    • Subject (SUBJ)
    • Object (OBJ)
    • Circumstancials (CIRC)
    • etc.
Dependency representations (and predicate-argument structures)
  • Dependencies
    • show which word depends on which word in a sentence
    • directed relations between two words in the sentence
    • Head / Governor -> Dependent / Modifier
  • Graph representations and not necessarily projective
  • Dependency Grammar
    • People: L. Tesnière, I. Mel’čuk, S. Starosta, J. Nivre
    • Paper: J. Nivre, Dependency grammar and dependency parsing,
      MSI report 05133. Växjö University, 2005.
    • Predicate argument structures
      • Predicate (PRED): words which denotes a process, a state or an action
      • Arguments (ARG): the necessary context that a predicate must have [obligatory and unique]
      • Modifiers (MOD): the circumstances under which the predicate instantiates in the sentence [facultative and possibly numerous].

Computer Science Representations

From linguistic point of view to computer science point of view
  • words or sentences or paragraphs or texts >> strings of characters or words
  • constituent representations >> trees
  • syntactic functions >> labeled graphs
  • dependency representations >> direct acyclic graphs (DAGs)
  • predicate-argument structures >> labeled direct acyclic graphs
Feature structures or attribute-value matrices (AVM)
  • to represent a list of attributes with their value, i.e., to represent trees and DAGs
  • relevant to the language at hand
  • specific to one language and not universal

Characteristics of Second generation MT

First Charateristic: Vauquois triangle (Analysis & transfer & generation)

>> Vauquois B, 1976


  • Unification grammar: reversible and bidirectional
  • A static grammar: specifies only the relationship between string and representation >> String-tree correspondence grammars (or here)
Step 1.1: Morphological analysis

  • Represent each word with feature structure

  • Tool: dictionary automata

    • Feature structures are associated with final states
    • Decomposition into several automata
    • Unification of two feature structures
      • Fail when incompatible

  • Output: a flat tree with factored ambiguities



Step 1.2: Structural analysis

Structural analysis is performed by sub-grammars that are applied one after another. Each sub-grammar contains rewriting rules that can be applied in parallel.

  • Rewriting rule

    • Left-hand and right-hand side (each has geometry (tree) and decoration pattern)



    • As feature structures



  • Output: a multi-level tree structure or an enormous feature structure that encapsulates different levels of analyses

    • logico-semantic representation: predicates, arguments modifiers
    • syntactic functions: subjects, objects, circumstancials
    • constituents: NP, VP, etc.
Step 2.1: Lexical transfer
  • Replace each lexical item by its equivalents
  • Disambiguation can be performed by looking at the context
  • Output: Unify the result of structural analysis with the bilingual lexicon (only the target part is interesting afterwards)
Step 2.2: Structural transfer

  • Covert the source language information the target language information (structure and attributes)

  • Proceed from high levels to low ones: high fail -> low applied

  • E.g., rewrite a noun with its support verb (oper1) with a corresponding verb



  • E.g., rewrite a noun complementized by an adjective with a verb and a circumstancial



Step 3.1: Structural generation
  • Inverse step of structural analysis
  • Output: a flat tree similar to the morphological analysis output (linear order structure that correspond to each element in the linear order)
Step 3.2: Morphological generation
  • Inverse step of morphological analysis
  • Output: the string, i,e., the sentence or the text

Second Charateristic: Specialized languages for linguistic programming

  • String-to-tree transformation: morphological analysis ([ATEF]([https://dash.harvard.edu/bitstream/handle/1/23492376/formalism%20and%20implementation%20of%20patr-ii%20-%20shieber%20-%201983.pdf?sequence=1](https://dash.harvard.edu/bitstream/handle/1/23492376/formalism and implementation of patr-ii - shieber - 1983.pdf?sequence=1)), automata languages)
  • Tree-to-tree transformations: structural analysis, lexical transfer, structural transfer, structural generation (PATR, ROBRA, GRADE)
  • Tree-to-string transformation: morphological generation

The use of feature structures is a nice example of declarative programming, by opposition to procedural programming.

Second generation MT systems

METEO

  • Context: Environment Canada
  • Name: TAUM-METEO, then METEO
  • Use: 1977-2001?
  • Characteristics: second generation MT system
  • Development: Automated Translation group at Montreal University (TAUM, Pierre Isabelle), then John Chandioux
  • Texts: Meteorological bulletins
  • Average sentence length: 18 ± 7 words
  • Amount of texts translated: 30 million words / year ∼ 120,000 pages / year
  • Efficiency: 7 times faster than translators
  • Quality: 11% of sentences to revise in 1985, 5% in 2000?

Research Systems

  • METAL developped at Texas U; and Siemens (Slocum), the basis of PROMT and Comprendium, no more known development
  • ARIANE developped at GETA (Vauquois, Boitet) no more known development, except as a spin-off: Lingwarium
  • ETAP 3 a research transfer-based system based on the Meaning-Text
    theory
  • Apertium an open research system initially designed to translate between
    close languages; the number of pairs is expanding

Commercial Systems 2000-2010

  • Systran v. 5 until the years 2010 or so
  • Reverso by Softissimo, supposed to be better than Systran for some language pairs according to an informal survey
  • PROMT by SailLabs(?) an MT engine that has been the engine of Reverso
    at some point in history
  • Comprendium rule-based and example-based systems
  • DUET [Sharp] and ATLAS I [Fujitsu]: semantic transfer
  • PIVOT [NEC] and ATLAS II [Fujitsu]: interlingua approach
  • Altflash [NTT]: rules and templates, stock exchange reports
  • SAKHR Arabic-English, a mix of 2nd generation and statistical approach

Main advantages

  • Several representation levels can be computed together
  • The translation unit can be more than a sentence
  • Analysis and generation are monolingual and can be reused for different language pairs

Main drawbacks

  • Rules requires a lot of manual work by qualified specialists
  • Maintenance is complex and difficult
  • Transfer process is specific for one language pair (directional and bilingual)
